# Raw research dump — Linear (worked example input)

> **Real company, public data.** This example profiles [Linear](https://linear.app) using its
> **public** voice-of-customer discussion — G2 reviews, "Linear vs Jira" comparisons and migration
> write-ups, and Product Hunt. Quotes are **representative and lightly edited from public
> discussion, with handles removed**; they illustrate patterns that recur across many posts, not any
> one individual. Sources are listed at the bottom.
>
> This dump was produced by the skill's **opt-in public-data sweep** (no first-party data was
> provided), the same way the Notion example was built. It exercises the **team-buyer** branch,
> where the account-ICP and buying-committee modules switch on.

**Product:** Linear, an issue-tracking and project-planning tool built for software teams. Known for
speed, keyboard-first design, and a developer-focused workflow. Motion is bottom-up (an engineering
team adopts it, then the org buys). **Top alternatives buyers name:** Jira (the incumbent), GitHub
Issues, Shortcut, Asana/ClickUp (for non-eng work).

---

## Source A — "Linear vs Jira" comparisons & migration write-ups (switching triggers)

- "Linear is fast in a way that makes you realize how slow Jira had become." Reported latency: under
  50ms for most actions in Linear vs. 800-3,000ms in Jira depending on workspace size.
- "Creating a bug report in Jira took ~48 seconds; the same report in Linear took ~11 seconds."
- "New developers need days to become proficient with Jira's interface and workflows; in Linear most
  reach full productivity within hours."
- "Cycles are lighter than sprints — set a duration, toss issues in, and incomplete work rolls into
  the next cycle automatically. No manual cleanup, no retro just to figure out what didn't get done."
- One team: "After a month, satisfaction with the issue tracker jumped from 3.2/10 to 7.8. The
  biggest driver wasn't any single feature — it was the feeling that the tool got out of the way."
- On migration cost: "We migrated 2,000 issues" — a real, non-trivial switching effort.

## Source B — G2 review excerpts (pros / cons)

- ★★★★★ *(Engineer):* "It's incredibly fast and responsive, which makes it easy to stay in the flow.
  Command-K lets you create issues and jump anywhere without leaving the keyboard."
- ★★★★★ *(Eng lead, startup):* "Integrates well with GitHub and is developer-friendly. Engineers can
  work right from their terminal without constantly context switching."
- ★★★★☆ *(Team lead):* "Gives everything a clear structure and flow — priorities and progress in one
  place, so we spend more time shipping instead of managing the tracker."
- ★★★☆☆ *(PM):* "Great for simplicity and speed, but it can feel too minimal if you need advanced
  reporting or highly customizable workflows. I miss being able to build custom dashboards like JQL
  in Jira."
- ★★★☆☆ *(Ops, mid-market):* "It's built for software development. Teams outside engineering find the
  interface too specialized."

## Source C — community & forum sentiment (barriers)

- "I'm slightly afraid Linear could become an enterprise tool one day — bloated and slow. Basically,
  afraid it becomes Jira."
- "Per-seat pricing gets expensive for larger orgs compared to tools with a forever-free tier."
- "Our whole company standardized on Atlassian. Getting eng onto Linear is easy; getting everyone
  else off Jira is the hard part."

## Source D — journey & discovery notes (from public discussion)

- Engineering teams discover Linear through its design-led reputation and word of mouth in dev and
  startup circles, X/Twitter, and Product Hunt.
- When actively evaluating, buyers read "Linear vs Jira" comparison and migration content.
- Adoption is **bottom-up**: an eng team tries it first and reaches the speed "aha" quickly, then the
  purchase decision moves up to eng leadership and finance.
