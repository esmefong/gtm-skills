# Frameworks

The methodology behind the skill. Read this when you need the full definition of a ring or a force,
or when you want to explain to a user *why* the workflow is shaped the way it is. Four methodologies,
each named because practitioners recognize them.

---

## 1. The 5 Rings of Buyer Insight (Adele Revella, Buyer Persona Institute)

The canonical structure that separates professional buyer research from template-filling. Each ring
is a dimension of the buying decision, and each is filled from what real buyers *say*, not from
internal opinion.

1. **Priority Initiative — "why now?"**
   The most compelling reason someone decides to invest *now* rather than later or never. The
   personal, professional, or organizational circumstances that made them reject the status quo and
   commit time, budget, or political capital. This is the "situation" in demand-side theory. It drives
   top-of-funnel messaging: if your hero copy doesn't name the trigger, you're talking to no one.

2. **Success Factors — "what does winning look like?"**
   The results the buyer needs to feel the purchase was worth it. Not what you think you deliver, what
   *they* need. Usually more emotional than product teams expect ("hold my own in the exec meeting,"
   "stop being the bottleneck," "look good to my board"). Drives benefit copy and success-story
   selection.

3. **Perceived Barriers — "what almost stopped you?"**
   The bad-news insight. What prevents purchase, why they doubt any solution will work, or why a
   competitor's approach looks safer. These exist whether you address them or not; the only choice is
   whether you answer them publicly. Drives FAQs, objection handling, guarantees, and testimonial
   selection.

4. **Decision Criteria — "how do you compare options?"**
   The specific capabilities buyers actually weigh when comparing you against alternatives. Not the
   criteria you wish they used. If 80% say one thing was decisive and 10% mention another, your
   messaging should reflect that ratio. Drives differentiation and comparison pages.

5. **Buyer's Journey — "where do you look and who do you trust?"**
   Every channel, resource, and person the buyer trusts as they move from "I might need this" to "I'm
   buying." Where they first heard of the category, where they researched, who they asked, what gave
   them confidence. Drives channel strategy, content distribution, and partnerships.

### The interview method

The 5 Rings are extracted from **unscripted** 30-45 minute recorded conversations with recent buyers
and near-buyers. The method matters as much as the questions:

- **One scripted question, then follow the story.** Open with: *"Take me back to the day you first
  decided you might need a solution like this. What was happening?"* From there, do not lead.
- **Probe with "what happened next?" and "why was that important to you?"** Not "why" as an
  interrogation, but as a way to keep the story moving and surface the emotional stakes. Map the journey
  by asking "what did you do next?" over and over.
- **Do not assume.** When they use a word, ask what they mean by it. The goal is their language, not a
  confirmation of yours.
- **Interview both sides of the decision.** Recent buyers (why they chose you), lost deals (why they
  didn't), and no-decisions (why they stayed put). The people who *didn't* buy carry the barriers you
  most need to hear.
- **Volume:** aim for ~8-10 interviews per segment before patterns stabilize; ~30 total for a
  multi-segment study. Record with permission, transcribe, tag within 48 hours.

## 2. Jobs-to-be-Done and the Four Forces (Bob Moesta, Clayton Christensen)

JTBD reframes the question from "who is the customer?" to "what progress are they trying to make?"
People don't buy products; they *hire* them to make progress in a specific situation. The job is the
progress. Your product is one vehicle for it.

### The Four Forces of Progress

A switch from the old way to the new only happens when the forces pulling toward change beat the forces
holding in place: **Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.**

- **Push** of the current situation — the dissatisfaction that makes the status quo untenable ("the
  spreadsheet finally broke," "we failed the audit").
- **Pull** of the new solution — the attraction of what's now possible ("this could just handle it").
- **Anxiety** of the new — fear about switching ("what if it doesn't integrate?", "what if I look
  foolish for championing it?").
- **Habit** of the present — the comfort and sunk cost of the current way ("we've always done it in
  Excel").

Marketers over-invest in Push and Pull (more features, louder benefits) and under-invest in Anxiety and
Habit. Reducing anxiety (proof, guarantees, migration help, a champion's internal cover) and breaking
habit is usually where a stalled deal is won.

### The Switch Interview

Interview people who **recently changed solutions** and walk backward through the decision forensically:
first thought → passive looking → the triggering event → active evaluation → the moment of decision →
first use. This timeline reveals the real trigger, which is almost never what the company assumes.

## 3. Demand-side selling (Bob Moesta, Ryan Singer)

The lens that keeps the whole exercise honest.

- **Demand-side thinking** asks: *what situation is this person in, what progress do they want, what
  tradeoffs will they accept?* **Supply-side thinking** asks: *how does my product's feature list map
  to a buyer?* Veteran marketers build demand-side ICPs. Weak ones write supply-side feature checklists
  dressed up as personas.
- **You sell into a situation, not to a persona.** Two buyers with identical LinkedIn profiles make
  opposite decisions because their situations differ. The ICP's job is to name the circumstances that
  make someone unable to *not* act, then position the product as the enabler of the progress they
  already want.
- **Practical consequence:** lead positioning with the buyer's world and the moment things shifted, not
  with your product. The narrative arc the buyer sees themselves in: "I was in this situation, I tried
  this alternative, it didn't work, I found this, now I've reached this success factor."

## 4. The buying committee (ABM-informed) — *only when the buyer is a team*

This whole section applies **only when a group buys** (most B2B and enterprise). For an individual
buyer — consumer, prosumer, lifestyle — skip it entirely; there is one buyer, and at most a gatekeeper
or approver to note. Don't manufacture a committee where there isn't one.

When a group does buy, an ICP that profiles one hero persona will resonate with the champion and get
killed by someone the champion never mentioned. Model the whole committee.

### The roles (appear in most committee deals)

- **Economic Buyer** — controls budget; cares about ROI and risk (often CFO, VP, or a budget owner).
- **Champion** — the internal seller; a mid-level operator who will fight for the deal but needs
  confidence and cover.
- **Technical Buyer** — evaluates integration, security, stability (CTO, IT, security lead).
- **End User** — lives in the tool daily; cares that it doesn't make their job harder.
- **Blocker / veto holder** — can kill the deal: procurement, legal, and in regulated industries,
  compliance and risk.

For each role, document: their KPI, their ring answers (especially their Perceived Barriers and
Decision Criteria differ sharply by role), their Four Forces, and the objection pattern. Then map the
**system**: who champions, who vetoes, who influences whom, and where deals stall.

### Account level vs. role level

Two distinct ICPs in B2B:

- **Account-level ICP** — *which companies* fit: firmographics (industry, size, revenue, geography),
  technographics (current stack, integration readiness), behavioral signals (recent funding, hiring in
  key functions, adjacent vendor switches), and **explicit disqualifiers**. This becomes a scoring
  rubric a rep answers in 30 seconds and a targeting list for ABM.
- **Role-level personas** — *which people* inside the account, per the committee roles above.

### Industry calibration

- **B2B SaaS:** full committee; PLG motions may compress to 1-3 stakeholders, enterprise SaaS expands.
- **Fintech / regulated:** add Compliance / Risk as a structural veto; cycles run 9-18 months; each
  added stakeholder lengthens the cycle; lead with risk mitigation and regulatory fit.
- **Enterprise:** procurement and security review as formal gates; multi-year cycles; heavy
  multi-threading.
- **Consumer / B2C:** no committee; the individual buyer plus any gatekeeper (a partner, a parent) or
  approver. Weight emotion and situation; find behavioral signals on social and in communities.
